## Abstract

We present and validate a single- and multi-signal **harmonic resonance** framework
that decomposes a time series into a per-frequency **harmonicity** spectrum H(f)
(how close the spectral peaks sit to integer ratios), a **phase-coupling** spectrum
PC(f) (how stably components hold an n:m phase relation), and their product, a
**resonance** spectrum R(f) = H(f)·PC(f), together with a panel of spectral-complexity
descriptors and an n_elec×n_elec connectivity extension. Our central claim is
methodological: harmonicity and phase coupling are **separable** axes that merit
measurement and reporting as distinct per-frequency spectra, and their product R = H·PC
is a principled, interpretable conjunction of the two — *not* a better detector. The
implementation is a **strategy registry** of swappable harmonic kernels, ratio kernels,
phase estimators, coupling metrics, and combine rules, making the choice of method an
explicit, testable variable; we build on established harmonicity and phase-coupling
measures and synchronization theory (see Related Work), contributing their unification,
validation, and tooling. We establish, in order: **(1)** the two factors are genuinely separable —
harmonicity is *exactly* phase-blind (ρ(H, phase-locking) = 0.00), while phase coupling
tracks locking; **(2)** the product R = H·PC is the principled conjunction — under
independently varied factors a conjunction beats *both* single factors (specificity AUC
≈0.99 vs ≤0.76) and beats disjunctions, and R adds a phase-coherence requirement that H
lacks (phase-scramble: H AUC 0.50 vs R 1.00) — though when the factors co-occur R does
not out-*detect* PC, so we present R as an interpretable decomposition, not a superior
detector; **(3)** the framework recovers known harmonic structure (ρ = 0.98) and n:m
coupling (AUC ≈ 1.0); **(4)** we characterize its operating range (detection vs SNR),
surrogate-null calibration, and runtime scaling; **(5)** it is competitive with
established measures (n:m PLV, harmonic-to-noise ratio); and we demonstrate **(6)**
method-choice/mechanism, **(7)** real-biosignal discrimination (matching band power),
**(8)** multichannel connectivity, and **(9)** the complexity descriptors as first-class
features. We are explicit about scope: the framework adds an interpretable
harmonic/coupling axis, and is **not** a replacement for band power on amplitude tasks.

## 1. Introduction

Biological time series are rich in *harmonic* structure: the QRS complex generates a
dense integer-harmonic series; cortical rhythms show cross-frequency relationships and
overtones; many physiological oscillators are weakly phase-coupled across frequency. A
unifying, interpretable way to quantify *how harmonically organized* a signal is — and at
which frequencies — would support comparison across states, modalities, and channels.

We formalize this as a **resonance spectrum**. For a power spectrum p(f), a harmonic
kernel S[i,j] scores the musical/harmonic simplicity of each frequency pair and a
phase-coupling kernel Φ[i,j] scores the n:m phase consistency of each pair. PSD-weighted
reductions give two per-frequency factors,

```
H(f_i)  = p(f_i) · Σ_j S[i,j] · p(f_j)          (harmonicity)
PC(f_i) = p(f_i) · Σ_j W[i,j] Φ[i,j] · p(f_j)    (phase coupling)
```

and their product R(f) = H(f)·PC(f) is the resonance spectrum. Each spectrum is
summarized by complexity descriptors (mean, max, spectral flatness, entropy, spread,
Higuchi fractal dimension, peak-harmonic-similarity). The implementation is a strategy
registry: harmonic kernels (`harmsim`, `subharm_tension`), ratio kernels (`binary`,
`fraction`), phase estimators (`stft`), coupling metrics (`nm_plv`, `nm_plv_canonical`,
`nm_pli`, `nm_wpli`, `nm_rrci`), and combine rules (`product`, `geomean`, `harmmean`,
`min`, `weighted_log`).

This paper validates the framework construct-first: we begin by establishing that H and
PC are *separable* and that the product R is *principled*, then validate recovery,
characterize operating range and baselines, and demonstrate applications — being explicit
about both what the framework does well and where it does not.

**The gap.** Existing practice offers no unified, *testable* harmonic axis. Harmonic
organization and phase coupling are typically reduced to separate scalar summaries; the
method choices that drive them (which harmonic kernel, ratio kernel, phase estimator,
coupling metric) are made implicitly and are therefore not comparable across studies; and
no toolbox reports per-frequency harmonicity and n:m phase coupling as *dissociable*
factors with a calibrated significance null and cross-signal/connectivity extensions. We
close this gap and validate the result construct-first.

### 1.1 Related work
**Harmonicity measures.** Quantifying "how harmonic is this spectrum?" has a long history
in audio/MIR and music perception: the harmonics-to-noise ratio (Boersma 1993), spectral
flatness/entropy (Johnston 1988), periodicity/relatedness harmonicity (Stolzenburg 2015),
template and spectral-peakiness models (Terhardt et al. 1982; Harrison & Pearce 2020), and
harmonic-similarity of interval sets (Gill & Purves 2009); number-theoretic ratio measures
(Euler's *gradus*, Tenney height, harmonic entropy) underlie the `harmsim` /
`subharm_tension` kernels we reuse from **biotuner** (Bellemare-Pepin & Jerbi 2025). That
harmonic organization is a *separable* factor — and a confound for cross-frequency coupling
when waveforms are non-sinusoidal — is established by **Dellavale et al. (2020)** (spectral
harmonicity / Time-Locked Index on nonlinear oscillators) and Aru et al. (2015); harmonic
relationships between EEG peaks are reported by Rodriguez-Larios et al. (2019). Our H is a
phase-blind, PSD-weighted, *per-frequency* spectralization of these scalar kernels, distinct
from the roughness axis (Plomp & Levelt 1965; Sethares 1993).

**Phase coupling, CFC, and inference.** n:m phase synchronization originates with Tass et
al. (1998), with broadband n:m cross-frequency synchrony shown in cortex by Palva et al.
(2005). Our coupling estimators are standard — PLV (Lachaux et al. 1999) and the
volume-conduction-robust PLI/wPLI/PPC family (Stam et al. 2007; Vinck et al. 2010, 2011).
PC measures phase-**phase** coupling, the rarer complement to the dominant phase-amplitude
paradigm (Canolty et al. 2006; Tort et al. 2010). Scheffer-Teixeira & Tort (2016) show that
phase-phase coupling claims are easily inflated by weak statistics; our factor-level
surrogate-z (AAFT/IAAFT; Theiler et al. 1992; Schreiber & Schmitz 1996) with mandatory
aperiodic removal (Donoghue et al. 2020) is a direct response. The swappable-metric +
surrogate-z software pattern is shared with **Tensorpac** (Combrisson et al. 2020) and
pactools (Dupré la Tour et al. 2017) — but those target phase-*amplitude* coupling, not an
n:m phase-phase comodulogram.

**Synchronization theory; consonance/FFR.** The generative result that ratio complexity
sets the n:m locking threshold (Arnold-tongue narrowing) is textbook nonlinear dynamics
(Arnold 1965; Pikovsky, Rosenblum & Kurths 2001; Glass & Mackey 1988); the a_n·a_m coupling
strength is a deliberate special case of weak-coupling phase reduction (Kuramoto 1984; Daido
1996; Ermentrout & Kopell 1991), and ratio complexity is already tied to brainstem
lockability by Large's mode-locking program (Lerud et al. 2014; Large et al. 2016). On the
auditory side, harmonicity is the load-bearing axis of consonance behaviorally (McDermott et
al. 2010; Bowling & Purves 2015) and neurally — Bidelman & Krishnan (2009) and Bidelman
(2013) recover the consonance ordering from FFR harmonic salience; intermodulation indexes
nonlinear interaction in FFR (Lee et al. 2009) and SSVEP (Boremanse et al. 2013; Norcia et
al. 2015). We *reproduce* these established effects as construct validation, not as new
findings.

### 1.2 Contributions
This is an **integration-and-validation** contribution; we are explicit about what is new
versus inherited.
1. A **swappable strategy registry** crossing harmonicity kernels with n:m phase-**phase**
   coupling metrics and combine rules, enabling an empirical kernel-vs-metric comparison —
   the toolboxes that share this software pattern (Tensorpac, pactools) target
   phase-*amplitude* coupling, so no off-the-shelf equivalent for n:m phase-phase resonance
   exists. *(strongest claim)*
2. An explicit, reusable per-frequency **R(f) = H(f)·PC(f)** descriptor that reports
   harmonicity and phase coupling as two separate spectra and their interpretable
   conjunction.
3. **Factor-level surrogate-z inference**, with the practical demonstration that testing the
   composite R can mask a real PC effect, so inference must target the factor that carries it.
4. **Cross-signal and multichannel connectivity** generalizations of the same operator.
5. A **construct-validity bridge**: we recover the classical Arnold-tongue dependence of
   locking on ratio complexity and use tongue width to validate the harmonicity factor.

The harmonicity construct, the n:m coupling and surrogate machinery, and the synchronization
theory are prior art (§1.1); our contribution is their unification, validation, and tooling.

## 2. Methods

**Resonance computation.** Single-signal analyses use the `compute_resonance` pipeline
(STFT phase estimator; per-frequency factor reduction; product combine) over a 2–45 Hz
band at 0.5 Hz resolution unless noted (0.5–45 Hz for ECG). The recommended configuration
pairs the `harmsim` harmonic kernel and the `fraction` ratio kernel with the
convention-correct `nm_plv_canonical` coupling metric. Cross-signal and connectivity
analyses use `compute_cross_resonance` / `compute_cross_resonance_connectivity`.

**What the coupling is measured on.** Phase coupling is a **phase–phase, polyrhythmic**
quantity computed between *pairs of spectral components*: the STFT phase estimator gives
the instantaneous phase θ(f,t) at each frequency bin, the ratio kernel picks the nearest
rational n:m of f_j/f_i, and the coupling metric measures how consistently the
generalized phase difference **n·θ_i − m·θ_j** stays fixed over time. This is evaluated
*within a signal* (cross-frequency coupling between its own peaks → the PC(f) spectrum),
*between two signals* (`compute_cross_resonance`), and *across channels* (the connectivity
matrix). It is therefore distinct from 1:1 coherence and from phase–amplitude coupling —
it detects n:m (polyrhythmic) phase relations between oscillatory components.

**Surrogate inference (factor-level).** Inference compares an observed spectrum to an
ensemble of phase-randomizing surrogates (AAFT for single signals; IAAFT for
cross-channel), forming a per-frequency z-score. Crucially, we z-score **each factor (H,
PC, R) separately** against the same surrogate ensemble, because R is H-dominated and
therefore PSD-driven under a power-preserving null; the phase-coupling factor PC is the
correct detector for n:m coupling and must be read as PC_z, not in absolute terms.

**Aperiodic removal is mandatory across conditions.** On pure colored noise, harmonicity
rises monotonically with the 1/f slope (a confound already noted for spectral harmonicity;
Donoghue et al. 2020); we remove the aperiodic component (`remove_aperiodic=True`) whenever
conditions differ in background 1/f. Removal fits a two-parameter power law P(f) = a·f^b to
the PSD over the analysis band (non-linear least squares) and subtracts it — a lightweight
aperiodic correction, *not* a FOOOF/specparam peak-decomposition.

**Signals & data provenance.** Synthetic generators with known structure (harmonic ladders,
n:m phase locks, polyrhythms, inharmonic/noise controls, independently-varied H×PC) on a
matched 1/f background (sampling 250–500 Hz, 12–40 s; SNR and seed grids per study). Real EEG
is the PhysioNet **EEG Motor Movement/Imagery** database (eegmmidb; Schalk et al. 2004),
auto-fetched via `mne.datasets.eegbci` (subjects 1–10): eyes-open vs eyes-closed from the two
baseline runs over occipital channels, rest vs motor from a baseline run vs a motor-execution
run over central channels, band-pass 1–45 Hz at the native 160 Hz. ECG/PPG/respiration are
simulated with neurokit2 (plus one bundled real ECG clip, tiled to length — noted where used).
**Default analysis parameters** (overridable via the registry): 0.5 Hz spectral resolution,
2–45 Hz band (0.5–45 Hz for ECG), `harmsim` harmonic kernel, `fraction` ratio kernel
(max-denominator 16), `nm_plv_canonical` coupling metric, `product` combine, aperiodic removal
on; STFT overlap is set per study. Detection/discrimination is scored by rank AUC (Mann–Whitney;
0.5 = chance) with percentile bootstrap 95% CIs (2000–3000 resamples, fixed seed); EEG decoding
is leave-one-subject-out; surrogate counts are 40–150 per study (more in `--paper`).

> **Reproducibility note.** The suite pins biotuner to the immutable commit
> `fc158c3` (full SHA in `requirements.txt`); the PyPI 0.4.1 build predates a
> phase-row alignment fix and silently breaks cross-frequency coupling detection at
> fmin ≥ 1 Hz. The version string is uninformative (`biotuner.__version__` reads
> 0.4.1 even on main), so we pin the SHA rather than rely on a version check.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 The construct: two separable factors *(Fig 1; Study 17)*
On a 2-D grid that independently varies ratio complexity (Tenney height) and
phase-locking strength κ, **harmonicity is exactly phase-blind**: ρ(H, κ) = **0.00** — H
is flat across locking and ordered only by ratio complexity. Phase coupling tracks
locking (ρ(PC, κ) = +0.49). The one honest asymmetry: PC is **not** ratio-blind
(ρ(PC, complexity) = −0.47), because stable mode-locking is physically easier at simple
ratios (the Arnold-tongue structure, §3.6). So H and PC are conceptually distinct
measurements — cleanly so for H — but not statistically independent in a real oscillator.
The construct is also confirmed **generatively** with the physically-correct phase model.
We couple harmonically-rich phase oscillators so that an n:m resonance is mediated by the
participating harmonics (oscillator-1's *n*-th meeting oscillator-2's *m*-th), giving it the
leading-order strength a_n·a_m (Pikovsky et al.). As coupling K rises each pair locks at its
n:m ratio and the framework's PC climbs through the synchronization transition — but the
**locking threshold K\* rises with ratio complexity** (K\* = 3.3, 8.7, 23.5 for 2:3, 3:4, 4:5;
ρ(K\*, n·m) = +1.0): complex ratios, mediated by higher and weaker harmonics, require stronger
coupling to lock. This is the Arnold-tongue narrowing (§3.6) seen in the *coupling-strength*
dimension. PC tracks this emergent locking directly, whereas the cross-spectral H responds only
weakly — and only because locking pulls the frequency onto the exact rational — so the framework's
phase coupling, not its harmonicity, reports dynamical synchronization (Fig 1D).

### 3.2 Why R = H·PC *(Fig 2; Study 23; generative panel C from Study 7)*
Two results justify the product. **(i) Operator analysis.** With H and PC sampled as
*independent* latent factors, a "true resonance" (both high) is separated from
single-factor confounds with specificity AUC **≈0.99 for conjunctions** (product 0.98,
geomean 0.98, harmmean 0.99, min 1.00) versus **0.75/0.75 for H/PC alone** and **0.66 for
the OR-disjunction (max)** — a conjunction is required, and beats both factors. (`min`
edges `product`; we adopt **product** because log R = log H + log PC gives an additive,
interpretable attribution of *why* a resonance is weak.) **(ii) The phase-coherence
gate.** Phase-scrambling a harmonic signal preserves its power spectrum exactly, so H is
*unchanged* (AUC coherent-vs-scrambled = 0.50, blind) while R collapses (AUC = 1.00): R
adds the phase-coherence requirement H lacks. **Honest scope:** when the factors co-occur
(coupled oscillators), R does not out-*detect* PC — its value is the interpretable
decomposition, not detection beyond PC. The gate also holds generatively: in the
harmonically-coupled 2:3 oscillators, R = H·PC stays low while harmonicity is present but the
phases are unlocked, and rises only as coupling brings the phases into n:m lock (Fig 2C) — the
dynamical analog of the phase-scramble.

### 3.3 Ground-truth recovery *(Fig 3; Studies 1, 5, 6)*
Mean harmonicity increases monotonically with known harmonic richness (Spearman ρ =
**0.98**); harmonic signals separate from inharmonic and from noise at AUC = **1.0**.
Surrogate-normalized coupling detection recovers known n:m locks: single-signal PC_z AUC
= **0.99**, phase-coupling matrix entry = **1.0**, and cross-signal 1:1/1:2/2:3 locks =
**1.0** each; polyrhythm (2:3:4) recovery = **1.0**.

### 3.4 Operating characteristics *(Fig 4; Study 24)*
Detection degrades gracefully with SNR: harmonicity detection holds at AUC ≈ 1.0 down to
**≈ −18 dB** (0.79 at −24 dB; chance below −30 dB), and n:m coupling has a sharp threshold
near **−12 dB**. The cross PC z-score is **approximately but not perfectly
null-calibrated**: on uncoupled pairs the per-instance false-positive rate is **≈ 0.09 at
α = 0.05** (n = 120; null z = 0.07 ± 1.07) — mildly anti-conservative, so a small
multiple-surrogate correction (or more surrogates) is advisable for strict type-I control.
Runtime is **dominated by the frequency-grid size, not signal length** (≈ flat in length;
≈ 60 ms at 43 bins → 1.1 s at 172 → 6.6 s at 430 bins), so cost is budgeted by
precision/band, not duration.

### 3.5 Baselines *(Fig 5; Study 25)*
Against established measures: framework **PC_z matches the oracle raw n:m PLV** for
coupling detection and is **more robust at low SNR** (≈6 dB lower detection threshold),
while additionally providing a calibrated surrogate null and auto-selecting the n:m ratio.
Framework **H matches the classical harmonic-to-noise ratio** at usable SNR (both AUC ≈
1.0 ≥ −18 dB); honestly, HNR is somewhat more noise-robust at extreme SNR (< −24 dB),
while H provides a per-frequency spectrum, handles multi-component/cross-signal cases, and
scores inharmonicity explicitly — capabilities HNR's single scalar lacks.

### 3.6 Strategy choice and mechanism *(Fig 6; Studies 4, 8)*
Sweeping the strategy registry, **detection accuracy saturates** (all 20 kernel×metric
configs ∈ [0.982, 1.0] on the recovery task) — so the practical differentiator is
**runtime**: `harmsim` is **~27× faster** than `subharm_tension` (0.21 s vs 5.64 s per
call) at equal accuracy. Mechanistically, we **recover the classical dependence of
lockability on ratio complexity** (Arnold 1965; Pikovsky et al. 2001): in a forced Van der
Pol oscillator, Arnold-tongue width correlates with ratio complexity (Spearman ρ = −0.50)
and with the framework's harmonicity (ρ = +0.41) — a sanity-check against established theory
that also supplies the physical basis for the H/PC coupling noted in §3.1. (We do not claim
this dependence as a finding; it is textbook synchronization theory used here to validate
the harmonicity factor.)

### 3.7 Real biosignals *(Fig 7; Studies 2, 3)*
On PhysioNet EEG (n = 10 subjects, leave-one-subject-out), eyes-open vs eyes-closed
decodes at AUC = **0.81**, **matching a relative-alpha-power baseline** (0.81) — an
interpretable harmonic-organization axis, not an improvement over band power. The
eyes-closed "more harmonic" effect is **1/f-dependent** (H_max AUC 0.83 with aperiodic
removal vs 0.29 without). On a rest-run vs motor-run contrast resonance reaches AUC =
**0.62** (permutation *p* = 0.001), modestly above the single-band-power baseline (0.54);
we flag, however, that this is a *run-level* (baseline-vs-task) comparison confounded by
arousal and time-on-task, **not** a clean event-locked motor decode, so we do not read it
as a genuine motor advantage. The full feature vector fingerprints **seven signal
modalities at 99%** (4-fold CV accuracy 0.99 ± 0.02; permutation *p* = 0.005; chance 14%),
best read as a multivariate signature on an intentionally easy separation.

### 3.8 The multichannel layer *(Fig 8; Study 21)*
The cross-resonance connectivity matrix recovers a planted coupled cluster (within-cluster
vs rest AUC: H 0.74, PC 0.80, R 0.81). The H matrix is diffuse and PC sharper; **R ≈ PC**
because H is near-uniform within the harmonic cluster (consistent with §3.2). **Honest
caveat:** the scalar reducer is broadband/overlap-biased, so recovery is driven partly by
shared spectra; isolating *pure* cross-channel phase coupling above a PSD-preserving null
requires the IAAFT surrogate-z variant (which isolates phase, as H does not survive a
power-preserving surrogate).

### 3.9 Spectral-complexity descriptors *(Fig 9; Study 22)*
The flatness/entropy/spread/Higuchi descriptors *of the H/PC/R spectra* are first-class
features: on signals of known structure they form a distinctive per-class "resonance
fingerprint," and they carry information the scalar summaries miss — H_avg conflates
pure-tone/inharmonic/narrowband/pink (all ≈ 0.17) while H-spectrum **flatness separates
them (0.17 → 0.93; harmonic-vs-noise AUC = 1.0)**.

## 4. Discussion

The framework is a **principled, separable, honestly-scoped** descriptor of harmonic
organization. Its two factors measure genuinely different things — harmonicity is exactly
phase-blind — and their product R is the natural conjunction (it beats both factors when
they vary independently, and gates harmonicity by phase coherence). The honest boundary is
that when harmonicity and locking co-occur (as in real coupled oscillators), R does not
out-detect PC; we therefore present R as an interpretable decomposition (log R = log H +
log PC attributes a weak resonance to its cause) rather than a superior detector.

Two methodological cautions generalize beyond this toolbox: (i) spectral harmonicity is
confounded by the **aperiodic (1/f) component** and must be computed after aperiodic
removal whenever a harmonicity *difference* is interpreted; and (ii) surrogate inference
must target the **factor that carries the effect** (PC for coupling), not a composite (R)
dominated by another factor (H). Scope: the framework adds value for harmonic and
cross-frequency-coupling structure, and matches — not beats — band power on
single-band amplitude tasks.

## 5. Limitations and reproducibility

**Limitations.** (1) *Detection parity.* When harmonicity and phase locking co-occur (real
coupled oscillators), R = H·PC does not out-detect PC; R is an interpretable decomposition
(log R = log H + log PC), not a detector beyond PC (§3.2, §3.8). (2) *Null calibration.* The
PC_z surrogate null is mildly anti-conservative (per-instance FPR ≈ 0.09 at α = 0.05), so use
more surrogates or a multiple-surrogate correction for strict type-I control (§3.4). (3)
*Connectivity reducer.* The scalar cross-channel reducer is broadband/overlap-biased; pure
cross-channel phase coupling requires the IAAFT surrogate-z variant (§3.8). (4) *Harmonicity
under power-preserving nulls.* Because H is fixed by the power spectrum, neither absolute H
nor absolute R can be read as harmonic *coupling* beyond what the PSD already implies; only
PC_z isolates n:m phase coupling under an AAFT/IAAFT null. (5) *No amplitude advantage.* On
single-band amplitude tasks the framework matches, not beats, band power (§3.7); the
rest/motor EEG contrast is additionally a confounded run-level comparison, not a clean motor
decode. (6) *Estimator scope.* The phase estimator is STFT-only; Hilbert/wavelet estimators
(registry slots present) are a natural next test. (7) *Strength of evidence.* Near-ceiling
detection AUCs (≈1.0) are on synthetic ground truth and index construct validity, not the
magnitude of coupling in real biosignals; the real-EEG effects are modest and reported as
such.

**Reproducibility.** Coupling detection and the connectivity reducer require the
alignment-fixed biotuner (pinned commit `fc158c3`, not PyPI 0.4.1; the version string is
uninformative). Quick-pass estimates are tightened by `--paper`. One command reproduces every
number (`python -m resonance_paper.run_all --paper`) and figure
(`python -m resonance_paper.paper.make_method_figures`); see §6.
